1.2 History of scientific publishing

  • Books
    • Historia Animalium (History of Animals) by Aristotle (384-322 BC)
    • Nicolaus Copernicus’ On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres (1543)
    • Li Shizhen (1518-1593) Encyclopedia of Chinese medicine (1596)
    • Johannes Kepler’s New Astronomy (1609)
    • Robert Hooke’s Micrographia (1635-1703)
    • Carolus Linneaus’ Species Plantarum (1753) and System Naturae (1758)
  • Correspondence
    • Galileo Galilei published a collection of his letters to other scientists in 1636
    • Letter to the Grand Duchess Christina
  • Public lectures and demonstrations
    • Followed by invited topics for publication by learned societies

1.3 The advent of scientific journals

  • Journal des Sçavans (1665-1792)

  • Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society of London (1665-present)

  • Proceedings of learned societies were bound together as a journal

  • Later it became possible to submit something without presenting it first

  • Peer review only started in mid-1900s

1.4 What is a scientific journal for?

Communication: - Journals record and disseminate the findings of individuals and teams of academics from all over the world - By having a date when they are published, together with the names of the authors, they record primacy - They attempt to register legitimacy by collating and integrating comments and concerns through peer review - They archive these findings so that in future people can build on the work
